Tokyo Transport Essentials — Read This First

Digital Suica / Pasmo Card Add to every phone's Apple Wallet or Google Pay before landing. All 8 people tap their phones at the turnstile — no paper tickets, no queuing at machines, no stress. Top up via app.
Google Maps — Your Navigator Google Maps Tokyo is extraordinarily precise. It tells you the platform number, which car to board (front/middle/rear), and the exact exit to use. Trust it completely. Download offline map before landing.
The Alphanumeric Code System Every station has a letter + number code (e.g., Shinjuku on the Marunouchi Line = M08). Train cars and platforms display these codes. If the numbers count up in your direction of travel, you're heading the right way.
Exit Numbers — Critical for Groups Tokyo stations are underground cities. Never just walk to "Exit." Google Maps will tell you the specific exit (e.g., "Exit A5" or "West Exit"). Wrong exit = 15–20 extra minutes of walking.
Car Position Strategy (8 people) Google Maps recommends "best car: middle" or "best car: rear." For a group of 8, always aim for middle cars — they're less crowded than stairway-adjacent cars. Spread across 2 doors of the same car.
Last Train Warning Tokyo trains stop running around midnight. If the 20-somethings stay out late in Shibuya or Shinjuku, they'll need to take a taxi or "GO" app (Japan's Uber). Budget ¥3,000–¥8,000 depending on distance.

Arrival & Shinjuku Neon

Monday · Arrival Day
You land in the late afternoon, so today is deliberately light. Your one job is to get checked in, get fed, and absorb that first electric-shock impression of Shinjuku at night — arguably the most intense neighborhood in Asia after dark. Everything tonight is walking distance from the hotel, so no subway needed.
4:15 PM
Arrive at Airport — Private Transfer Waiting
Clear customs and collect luggage. Your private driver will be waiting in the arrivals hall — look for a sign with your family name. This is the easiest possible airport transfer for a group of 8 with luggage: no tickets, no turnstiles, no train navigation while jet-lagged. The transfer to Keio Plaza Hotel in Shinjuku takes approximately 60–90 minutes from Narita (or 45–60 minutes from Haneda) depending on traffic. Use the ride to decompress, share first impressions of Japan out the window, and get your phones connected to Japanese data (either a local SIM or pocket WiFi — arrange this in advance; both are available in the arrivals hall if needed).
On the Ride: Look for the first glimpses of Japanese urban life — the immaculate roadways, the organized signage, the vending machines lit up on every corner. It begins to sink in that you are somewhere very different from home.
6:30 PM
Check in — Keio Plaza Hotel Tokyo
The Keio Plaza is an ideal base: it's directly connected to Shinjuku Station's west exit, it has English-speaking staff, and it sits roughly 10 minutes walk from everything you'll do tonight. Get settled, freshen up, and gather the group. Tonight's plan is entirely on foot.
8:00 PM
Dinner — Omoide Yokocho (Memory Lane)
A narrow alley tucked under the elevated train tracks, Omoide Yokocho ("Memory Lane" or sometimes called "Piss Alley," historically) is one of Tokyo's most atmospheric spots. Tiny yakitori bars with 6–8 seats each line both sides, lanterns glow, smoke curls from charcoal grills, and the smell of grilled chicken and beer is everywhere. This is not a single restaurant — it's a collection of tiny stalls. Split your group of 8 into pairs and let everyone choose a stall. Order yakitori skewers (chicken, liver, cartilage, leek), edamame, and cold Sapporo or Asahi. This is "showa era" Tokyo — pure nostalgia for a city that barely exists anymore.
Ordering Tip: Point at items on the skewers hanging near the grill and hold up fingers for quantity. Most stalls don't have English menus but are extremely tourist-friendly. Budget ¥2,000–¥3,500 per person including drinks.
9:30 PM
Evening Stroll — Shinjuku Golden Gai & Kabukicho
From Memory Lane, walk 5 minutes to Golden Gai — a labyrinthine network of six tiny alleys packed with some 200 intimate bars, each holding 5–8 people. Many are themed (jazz bars, horror film bars, bars that only play one artist). Some charge a "cover fee" of ¥500–¥1,000 per person, but it's worth it for the uniqueness. Your adult children will love this. Then walk another 5–10 minutes into Kabukicho — Japan's most famous red-light and entertainment district. It's safe to walk through, and the scale of neon is genuinely staggering. Look up to find the giant animatronic Godzilla head perched atop the Shinjuku Toho Building — it occasionally roars and lights up.
Alternative / If Jet-Lagged
Shinjuku Gyoen Garden (if illuminated): During cherry blossom season, Shinjuku Gyoen sometimes offers evening illuminations until 8 PM. Check ahead — a quiet walk among lantern-lit cherry trees is a beautiful antidote to jet lag.
Early night: No shame in calling it at 9 PM on arrival day. You have four full days ahead and cherry blossoms that won't wait.

Ueno · Asakusa · Skytree

Tuesday · East Tokyo & Old Shitamachi
Today takes you to the older, eastern half of Tokyo — the "shitamachi" (downtown) neighborhoods that survived WWII bombing and carry the spirit of Edo-era city life. You'll move from the grand cultural institutions of Ueno, to the ancient thunder gate of Asakusa, to the soaring heights of Tokyo Skytree at sunset. This is the historic Tokyo, and April is arguably its most beautiful month — cherry blossoms line the Sumida River and Ueno Park simultaneously.
8:30 AM
Ueno Park — Cherry Blossom Viewing
Ueno Park is Japan's most famous hanami (flower viewing) spot. During peak bloom (which you should hit perfectly in early April), the 1,200 cherry trees form a nearly continuous canopy of blush pink above the main paths. On weekday mornings it's still relatively calm; on weekends and afternoons it becomes a jubilant outdoor party with thousands of people picnicking under the trees. Walk the main promenade past the outdoor stages, past the temple and pagoda, and up toward the Tokyo National Museum. This is the mood-setter for your entire Japan trip — a reminder of why you came in spring.
From Keio Plaza Hotel: Walk to Shinjuku Station West Exit. Take the JY JR Yamanote Line (Green Loop) Northbound from Shinjuku [JY17] → Ueno [JY05]. About 30 minutes, 9 stops. Look for green JR signs (elevated train, not underground). No transfers. Get off at Ueno Station and use the Park Exit (公園口).
9:30 AM
Tokyo National Museum (Ueno Park)
The Tokyo National Museum is the oldest and largest art museum in Japan, and its main building (Honkan) is worth at least an hour. The collection spans 110,000 objects — samurai armor and swords, lacquerware, ceramics, Buddhist sculpture, and screen paintings spanning 2,000 years of Japanese culture. For a group that will spend three days in Kyoto's temples, this provides essential context: you'll understand what you're looking at when you encounter a Bodhisattva or a Mon family crest on a castle wall. The building itself, a 1938 Imperial Style structure, is beautiful. Focus on floors 1 and 2 of Honkan; skip the Heiseikan and Toyokan unless you have extra time.
Time Guidance: 60–90 minutes is ideal. The museum opens at 9:30 AM. Book tickets online to skip the queue (especially on weekends).
11:30 AM
Lunch — Ramen Kamo to Negi
Near Okachimachi Station (one stop south of Ueno on the Yamanote Line), Ramen Kamo to Negi is a celebrated ramen shop specializing in rich duck broth (kamo = duck, negi = green onion). The bowls are clear, elegant, and intensely savory — a very different style from the thick tonkotsu ramen most Westerners know. This is artisanal ramen at its best. For a group, go when it opens for lunch to avoid the queue.
Walk or 1-stop train: From Ueno Park, it's a 12-minute walk south to the Okachimachi area, or take the JR Yamanote Line one stop south to Okachimachi Station.
1:00 PM
Asakusa — Sensō-ji Temple & Nakamise-dori
Asakusa is the spiritual and cultural heart of old Tokyo, and Sensō-ji is its crown jewel — Tokyo's oldest temple, founded in 628 AD. Enter through the towering Kaminari-mon (Thunder Gate), with its iconic giant red lantern and the fierce guardian deities Fujin and Raijin flanking the entrance. Walk the 250-meter Nakamise-dori shopping arcade — lined with stalls selling ningyo-yaki (little sponge cakes filled with sweet bean paste), ningyo dolls, folding fans, daruma, and seasonal sakura-themed sweets. The main hall and five-story pagoda are beautifully photogenic, especially with blossoms. Allow time to draw an omikuji fortune (¥100), and explore the smaller Asakusa Shrine next door.
From Ueno to Asakusa: Take the G Tokyo Metro Ginza Line (Orange) Eastbound from Ueno [G16] → Asakusa [G19]. 3 stops, about 7 minutes. Look for the orange "G" signs at Ueno. Exit at Exit 1 — you emerge directly facing Kaminari-mon Gate.
In the Area — Alternatives & Add-ons
Sumida Park: A 5-minute walk from the temple, this riverside park offers one of Tokyo's most photographed cherry blossom scenes — blossoms reflected in the Sumida River with Skytree rising behind them. Don't miss this if blossoms are at peak.
Asahi Brewery HQ (the "Golden Flame" building): The sculptural gold blob atop the Asahi Beer building across Azuma Bridge is one of Tokyo's most iconic (and debated) pieces of architecture. Walk across Azuma Bridge for the Skytree + Asahi Building + cherry blossom photo — one of the best compositions in the city.
Nakamise Craft Shops (side streets): The alleys behind Nakamise-dori hide authentic craft shops selling indigo-dyed textiles, handmade combs, traditional footwear (geta), and kanzashi hair ornaments. Slower and more genuine than the main arcade.
Sumida Aquarium (inside Skytree complex): If it rains, the aquarium in the Skytree's East Tower is a good 90-minute backup activity with a beautiful jellyfish display.
Edo-Tokyo Museum (Ryogoku): One of the great history museums in Japan, located in Ryogoku — just one stop past Asakusa on the Toei Oedo Line, or a 15-minute walk from Skytree. The museum tells the complete story of Tokyo from Edo-period fishing village to modern megalopolis, with full-scale reproductions of Edo streets, a replica of the original Nihonbashi bridge, and extraordinary scale models of the city after the 1923 earthquake and WWII firebombing. If the weather turns poor or someone in the group wants a deep-dive into Japanese history, this is the ideal rainy-day alternative — budget 2 hours minimum. Note: confirm it has re-opened after its post-2022 renovation before your trip.
3:30 PM
Walk Azuma Bridge → Tokyo Skytree
Walk 15 minutes from Sensō-ji across the Azuma Bridge — this is where you'll stop for the quintessential photo: Skytree reflected in the river, surrounded by blossoms, with the gold Asahi flame visible to the left. It's a genuinely iconic composition. Then continue to the Skytree's base. The East and West towers of the Skytree complex contain Solamachi, a shopping mall with over 300 shops, if anyone wants to browse before the timed observation deck entry.
5:30 PM
Tokyo Skytree — Observation DeckHard Point
At 634 meters, Tokyo Skytree is the tallest structure in Japan and the second tallest in the world. Your timed entry is at 5:30 PM — this is the ideal time to experience both the daylight panorama (still visible at 5:30 in April) and the magic of watching the city lights emerge as the sun sets around 6:10 PM. The Tembo Deck at 350m is included in the base ticket; the Tembo Galleria at 450m (with its glass-bottomed walkway) requires an additional ticket purchased on-site. On a clear day you can see Mt. Fuji to the southwest — ask a staff member which direction to look. The skyline of metropolitan Tokyo stretching 70km in every direction is one of the most staggering sights in the world.
Photo Strategy: The best Mt. Fuji views are from the northwest-facing windows on the upper deck. At dusk, the LED displays on the Skytree itself change color — look for the "Edo Blue" (aizome) and "Edo Purple" (murasaki) illumination schemes, both inspired by traditional Japanese colors.
7:30 PM
Dinner & Return to Shinjuku
Solamachi (the Skytree mall complex) has excellent dining options on its 6th and 7th floors — Japanese, sushi, ramen, tonkatsu — perfect for a group with varied tastes after a long day. Alternatively, return to Shinjuku for Kirin City (lively beer hall) or explore more of the Shinjuku restaurant floors of Takashimaya Times Square.
Skytree (Oshiage) → Shinjuku: Take the Z Hanzomon Line (Purple) Westbound from Oshiage [Z14] → Otemachi [Z08] (6 stops). Transfer to the M Marunouchi Line (Red) Westbound from Otemachi [M18] → Shinjuku [M08]. Total journey about 40 minutes. Follow purple "Z" signs at Oshiage, then red "M" signs at Otemachi.

Harajuku · Meiji Jingu · Shibuya

Wednesday · Culture, Fashion & The Crossing
Today is the most "contemporary Tokyo" day — you'll move from a 100-year-old forest shrine in the middle of the city to the world's most watched fashion street, then to the global crossroads of Shibuya. It's a day that gives your 20- and 30-something group room to split up and explore at their own pace. The walk from Harajuku to Shibuya is only 15 minutes and is one of Tokyo's most interesting urban strolls.
9:00 AM
Meiji Jingu Shrine & Forest
Meiji Jingu is one of the most important Shinto shrines in Japan, dedicated to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken. But it's the forest that makes it extraordinary: 70,000 trees were planted when the shrine was completed in 1920, donated from every corner of Japan. Today it's a 175-acre secondary-growth forest — still, birdsong-filled, and psychologically the opposite of everything surrounding it. The path from Harajuku Station to the main shrine takes 10–12 minutes through tall cedars and the imposing torii gate. The Main Hall features the traditional shamusho (offering hall) and is the site of real ceremonies. Pick up a wooden ema board (¥500) to write a wish on and hang it with thousands of others. The attached Meiji Jingu Museum holds personal effects of the imperial couple — understated but fascinating.
From Shinjuku to Harajuku: Walk to Shinjuku Station. Take the JY JR Yamanote Line (Green) Southbound (Outer Loop) from Shinjuku [JY17] → Harajuku [JY19]. 2 stops, about 5 minutes. Use Omotesando Exit — you exit directly facing the shrine's main torii gate.
Etiquette at the Shrine: Bow slightly when passing through the torii gates. At the temizuya (water basin near the entrance), rinse your hands: left hand first, then right, then cup water to rinse your mouth, then rinse the ladle handle. This is the purification ritual before approaching the gods.
11:00 AM
Takeshita Street — Harajuku Fashion
Exit the shrine and cross the road to Takeshita-dori (Takeshita Street) — a 350-meter pedestrian street that is the origin point of Japan's youth fashion subcultures. Harajuku street fashion once gave birth to Lolita, decora, and Visual Kei styles that influenced designers worldwide. Today it's more commercialized but still delivers: crêpe stands, hyper-decorated clothing shops, kawaii accessory stores, and a density of young fashion energy you won't find anywhere else. The 20- and 30-somethings will know exactly what they want here. For the parents: treat it as anthropology and try a matcha crêpe. Then when Takeshita feels complete, walk two blocks east to Cat Street — an unmarked alley between Harajuku and Omotesando that features independent boutiques, vintage stores, concept shops, and the more mature, curated side of Tokyo street fashion.
In the Area — Alternatives
Yoyogi Park (adjacent to Meiji Jingu): During hanami season, Yoyogi Park is where young Tokyo goes to party under the cherry trees — bento boxes, guitar players, cosplayers, and picnic blankets as far as you can see. A legitimate 45-minute detour before Takeshita Street.
Omotesando Hills & Avenue: For high-end shopping (Louis Vuitton, Dior, the beautiful Prada building by Herzog & de Meuron), walk south from Cat Street to Omotesando Avenue — Tokyo's equivalent of the Champs-Élysées, but greener and more elegant.
Nezu Museum: A quiet, world-class collection of Asian art with a beautiful bamboo garden, tucked into Minami-Aoyama. A peaceful counterpoint if the Harajuku crowds feel like too much.
1:00 PM
Walk to Shibuya — The Famous Crossing
Walk 15 minutes south from Harajuku through the boutiques of Omotesando to Shibuya. This is one of the great urban walks in Asia. As you enter the Shibuya scramble intersection area, look for the elevated viewing position at the Starbucks second floor (overlooking the crossing) or better yet, the SHIBUYA CROSSING S VIEW at Mag's Park / Scramble Square observation deck on the 11th floor — the best bird's-eye view of the crossing available without paying for a full observation deck. At peak times, over 3,000 people cross simultaneously from all directions, then the intersection clears in 45 seconds. It's mesmerizing and like nothing else in the world.
Don't Miss: The statue of Hachiko the loyal dog is right outside Shibuya Station's Hachiko Exit — one of Japan's most beloved stories and a classic meeting point. Great for a group photo.
2:30 PM
Shibuya Shopping & Afternoon Exploration
Shibuya 109 (the cylindrical building — specifically the men's version, 109 Men's, is popular for streetwear) and GU Shibuya (Uniqlo's fashion-forward sibling brand) are good bets for the younger group. Loft Shibuya is an exceptional Japanese lifestyle and stationery store — six floors of the most beautifully designed everyday objects you've ever seen. For the adults who want to experience how Japan thinks about design in everyday life, Loft is genuinely revelatory. If the group wants to split: parents can find a cafe while the kids shop.
6:30 PM
Dinner — Shibuya Options
Hikiniku to Come is one of the most talked-about restaurants in Tokyo — a minimalist shop serving a single item: a perfectly formed, loosely packed hamburger steak (hikiniku = minced meat) with a runny egg on a bowl of rice. Reservations are essential and highly competitive. If you can't get in, Soba MAREN Shibuya (from your list) offers elegant handmade soba in a more accessible setting. For a more casual large-group dinner, the upper floors of Shibuya Hikarie or Mark Is have numerous options.
Return to Shinjuku: From Shibuya Station, take the JY JR Yamanote Line (Green) Northbound from Shibuya [JY20] → Shinjuku [JY17]. 2 stops, 5 minutes. Or walk the 2km if the evening is pleasant — it's an interesting night walk through Sendagaya.
Evening Options for the 20s/30s Group
Bar Nayuta (Shibuya): From your list — a craft cocktail bar that's earned a dedicated following for its creative Japanese-inflected drinks.
oh 釜 bar shibuya: Another from your list — a more casual sake and shochu bar experience.
Shimokitazawa: If any of the group are into vintage clothing, vinyl records, and underground live music, Shimokitazawa is 5 minutes from Shibuya on the Keio Inokashira Line — one of Tokyo's most beloved bohemian neighborhoods.

Cherry Blossoms · Palace · teamLab · Ginza

Thursday · The Grand Arc — Sakura to Immersive Art to Luxury
Today is the arc from ancient to ultramodern. You begin at Chidorigafuchi — the most celebrated cherry blossom moat in Tokyo — then walk south through Kitanomaru Park to the Imperial Palace perimeter for the classic Nijubashi Bridge photo. A short subway hop takes you to Shiodome in time to watch the Nittele Giant Clock's noon performance — a Hayao Miyazaki-designed mechanical marvel that is one of Tokyo's most unexpected delights. From there it's a quick ride to teamLab Borderless at 1:00 PM, followed by a leisurely late afternoon in Ginza. A full day, but with a perfect geographic flow from northwest to southeast across the city.
8:30 AM
Chidorigafuchi Moat — The Great Sakura Walk
Chidorigafuchi is the northwest moat of the Imperial Palace grounds, and during peak cherry blossom season it produces one of Japan's most iconic images: hundreds of weeping sakura branches reaching down over jade-green water, with small rowboats drifting beneath them. This is the most beautiful thing you will see in Tokyo. The 700-meter Chidorigafuchi Greenway pedestrian path runs along the moat's edge — walk it slowly. You can rent rowboats at the Chidorigafuchi Boathouse (approximately ¥800 for 30 minutes per boat, 2 people per boat — so budget for 4 boats for the group). If you want boats, arrive early — before 9:00 AM if possible. Lines grow quickly after 9:30 AM and can reach 1.5–2 hours. The walk itself is spectacular even without a boat.
From Shinjuku to Kudanshita: Take the S Toei Shinjuku Line (Leaf Green) Eastbound from Shinjuku [S01] → Kudanshita [S05]. 4 stops, about 12 minutes. Look for leaf-green "S" signs at Shinjuku Station (this is a Toei line, not a Tokyo Metro — still works with Suica). Use Exit 2 — you emerge at the top of the moat path near Yasukuni Shrine.
Boat Strategy for 8 People: You'll need 4 boats (2 people each). Everyone goes to the boathouse together, splits into pairs, and launches. Stay within sight of each other on the water — the moat isn't large, but it gets busy. The 30-minute session goes quickly; 45 minutes is the sweet spot if they offer it.
11:00 AM
Imperial Palace Perimeter Walk & Nijubashi Bridge
From Chidorigafuchi, walk south through Kitanomaru Park (itself a beautiful cherry blossom spot — the National Museum of Modern Art is here if anyone wants to split off for 30 minutes). Continue south to the main Imperial Palace viewing area. The palace complex is not open to the public in the regular sense, but the outer grounds and the view of Nijubashi Bridge are completely accessible and iconic. The Nijubashi (literally "double bridge") and its stone arch reflected in the palace moat, framed by the white Fushimi Turret, is the classic Tokyo postcard photograph — the image of the capital. Allow 10–15 minutes for photos. Then stroll through the East Gardens, which are free to enter and contain the massive stone foundations of Edo Castle, Japan's largest feudal fortress. The scale of the stone ramparts will genuinely surprise you.
Nearby Alternatives
Marunouchi district: The approach from the palace east toward Tokyo Station is lined with Meiji-era brick buildings converted to high-end retail (Kitte, Marunouchi Building) — Japan's version of a historic business district.
Mitsubishi Ichigokan Museum: A stunning red-brick Western-style 1894 museum reconstruction with the beautiful Café 1894 inside — perfect for a coffee stop before the subway to teamLab.
Hibiya Park: Just south of the palace, often hosting spring food festivals and picnics — good quick detour en route to the subway.
11:45 AM
Nittele Giant Clock — Shiodome (Nippon TV Tower)
From the palace, head to Shiodome — a short subway hop — to catch the noon performance of the Nittele Giant Clock, one of Tokyo's most delightful surprises. This colossal 12-meter-tall mechanical clock is mounted on the exterior of the Nippon Television (NTV/Nittele) headquarters building, designed by legendary animator Hayao Miyazaki of Studio Ghibli. It looks exactly as you'd imagine — gears, moving figures, steam valves, dangling pendulums, and fantastical creatures that come to life on the hour. The full choreographed show runs at 12:00 PM sharp on weekdays (also 3:00 PM, 6:00 PM, and 7:00 PM). Arrive by 11:50 AM to get a good spot in front. The show lasts about 3–4 minutes and is genuinely magical — especially for anyone who grew up on Ghibli films. The surrounding Caretta Shiodome complex has cafés if you need a quick coffee before teamLab. Allow 30 minutes total here.
Palace area (Hibiya) → Shiodome: Walk to Hibiya Station. Take the H Hibiya Line (Gray) Southbound from Hibiya [H07] → Tsukiji [H12] (4 stops, about 8 minutes). Then walk 8 minutes west to Shiodome/Caretta Shiodome — or alternatively take one stop on the Yurakucho Line to Shimbashi and walk 5 minutes. The Nippon TV tower and clock are visible from the street level as you approach.
Ghibli Connection: Miyazaki designed the clock as a personal gift to the city — it carries the same hand-crafted, whimsical spirit as his films. Point out the small characters and figures tucked into the mechanism — there are more details than you'll catch at first glance. This stop takes almost no time but generates outsized delight.
12:30 PM
Transit: Shiodome → teamLab Borderless
Shiodome is conveniently close to Kamiyacho/Azabudai Hills. Head to teamLab with a comfortable buffer before your 1:00 PM entry.
Shiodome → teamLab Borderless (Azabudai Hills): Walk 8 minutes to Shimbashi Station. Take the H Hibiya Line (Gray) Southbound from Shimbashi [H09 — note: confirm current code] → Kamiyacho [H05]. About 3 stops, 6 minutes. Kamiyacho Station connects directly via underground walkway to Azabudai Hills. Follow signs to Azabudai Hills Mori JP Tower — teamLab is on the basement levels. Total transit time from clock to teamLab: about 20 minutes.
1:00 PM
teamLab Borderless — Azabudai HillsHard Point
teamLab Borderless is a 10,000 square meter museum where the digital artworks are "borderless" — they flow between rooms, respond to visitors, and change in real time. This is the re-opened version in Azabudai Hills (the original Odaiba venue closed in 2024 for this new, larger space). There are no fixed rooms or set routes — the artworks change and evolve, and some are ephemeral. Expect rooms where your footsteps generate blooming flowers, where light cascades through waterfalls that reform when you touch them, where you walk through kaleidoscopic universes of interconnected creatures. The most popular installation typically involves lying on the floor in a room of shifting constellations. Budget a minimum of 2 hours; your group will likely want 2.5–3 hours. Wear dark clothing if possible — it maximizes the immersive effect. The onsite café is atmospheric and worth a break mid-visit.
Photography Note: Everything is designed to be photographed and shared — go wild. The light installations photograph best at low ISO with a longer exposure; phones in "night mode" handle this well. Some of the best shots are low-angle shots with the light streaming above you.
4:30 PM
Ginza — Tokyo's Most Refined District
Ginza is Tokyo's answer to Paris's 8th arrondissement — orderly, luxurious, and impeccably maintained. The main boulevard (Chuo-dori) on weekends becomes a pedestrian street. Key stops: GINZA SIX is the district's flagship building — 13 floors of extraordinary retail, a rooftop garden, and basement food hall that is a destination in itself. Ginza Loft (design goods and stationery). Age.3 GINZA is the place for its famous deep-fried fruit sandwiches (sando) — a uniquely Japanese dessert snack. Itoya, across the street, is a nine-floor stationery wonderland. For the parents, the Okura Hotel's bar (a 10-minute walk) or the Mandarin Oriental's 37th-floor bar are exceptional for an evening cocktail.
teamLab (Kamiyacho) → Ginza: Take the H Hibiya Line (Gray) Northbound from Kamiyacho [H05] → Ginza [H09]. 3 stops, about 6 minutes. Exit at Exit A2 or A3 which puts you on Chuo-dori, the main Ginza boulevard.
Roppongi Detour — If the Group Has Energy
Tokyo Tower: A 10-minute walk from teamLab — the 1958 red-and-white transmission tower is still beloved and photogenic, especially at dusk when it glows orange against the darkening sky. Skip the observation deck (Skytree is more impressive) but the ground-level photo is worth it.
Roppongi Hills Sky Deck: The open-air observation deck on Mori Tower gives a panoramic city view with a different angle than Skytree — good if anyone wants yet another urban panorama.
National Art Center, Tokyo: Japan's largest exhibition space (nearest station: Nogizaka) — changing exhibitions in a building with a dramatic undulating glass facade.
7:00 PM
Farewell Tokyo Dinner — Ginza
Ginza Tenryu is famous for its gyoza (the family-sized portions are legendary) — a lively, no-frills classic that fits 8 people comfortably and won't break the bank. For a more elevated farewell dinner, Japanese Restaurant Kiwagiwa Ginza offers a refined kaiseki-influenced menu. Either way, this is your last night in Tokyo before departing for Kyoto — make the dinner count. Toast to the cherry blossoms, the Skytree, the crossing, the moat.
Return to Shinjuku from Ginza: Take the M Marunouchi Line (Red) Westbound from Ginza [M16] → Shinjuku [M08]. Direct, no transfers, about 18 minutes. This is the easiest route home. Exit at West Exit (Nishi-guchi) for the Keio Plaza.

Tsukiji · Tokyo Station · Shinkansen to Kyoto

Friday · Final Morning & Departure
A shorter day — you're departing for Kyoto. The morning is designed around Tsukiji Outer Market, where Japan's professional food culture is on full display, and then an efficient run back to the hotel and on to Tokyo Station for the Nozomi bullet train. Pack bags the night before.
7:30 AM
Tsukiji Outer Market — Breakfast of Champions
The Tsukiji Inner Market (tuna auctions) moved to Toyosu in 2018, but the Outer Market remains one of Tokyo's greatest food experiences. Over 60 stalls and small restaurants line narrow alleys selling: fresh uni (sea urchin) on crackers, tamago-yaki (the sweet rolled egg omelette, made to order and eaten on a skewer), grilled scallops, otoro (fatty tuna) sashimi over rice, fresh oysters, and tamagoyaki sando. This is breakfast like nowhere else in the world. The market opens around 5 AM but is best visited 7:30–9:30 AM. Walk and eat — this is not a sit-down meal. Budget ¥2,000–¥3,500 per person for a full tasting stroll. Don't miss Tamagoya (egg omelette) and Daiwa Sushi (one of the few remaining sushi counters in the outer market).
From Shinjuku to Tsukiji: Take the E Toei Oedo Line (Magenta) Eastbound from Shinjuku [E01] → Tsukiji-shijo [E18]. Direct, about 20 minutes, no transfers. Look for magenta "E" signs at Shinjuku. Use Exit A1 which puts you at the market entrance.
Nearby — If Time Permits
Tsukiji Hongan-ji Temple: An extraordinary 1934 Buddhist temple built in an Indo-Saracenic architectural style (domes, elephant sculptures, Sanskrit inscriptions) — entirely unlike any other temple you'll see in Japan. A 3-minute walk from the market. Worth 15 minutes even if you're not religious.
Hamarikyu Gardens: A 17th-century tidal garden between Tsukiji and the bay — free if you use the garden entrance before the pay gate opens. The contrast of ancient pines and tidal ponds against the Shiodome skyscrapers is striking. Budget 30 minutes if departing late.

11:30 AM
Private Transfer — Keio Plaza to Tokyo Station — Nozomi Shinkansen to Kyoto
Your private driver will collect the group from the Keio Plaza Hotel lobby and transfer you directly to Tokyo Station — no subway navigation with loaded luggage, no turnstile stress. Confirm the pickup time with the driver the evening before based on your Shinkansen departure. The hotel-to-station drive is approximately 25–35 minutes depending on traffic; the driver will drop you at the Shinkansen entrance side of the station (the Marunouchi side, with the famous red-brick facade). Allow 20 minutes inside the station to locate your platform (14–19 for the Tokaido Shinkansen), validate tickets, and find your reserved cars. The Nozomi Shinkansen to Kyoto takes approximately 2 hours 15 minutes — Kyoto is the third major stop after Shinagawa and Nagoya. Book reserved seats in advance as a group (8 seats, two rows of 4, or across the aisle in the same car). The Nozomi is the fastest service and not covered by the standard JR Pass — if you purchased passes, you'll need the Hikari service instead (30 minutes longer). Views of Mt. Fuji appear from the right side of the train (A, B, C seats) roughly 40–50 minutes after departure.
Fuji Photo Strategy: Sit on the right side (A, B, C seats), cameras ready about 40 minutes after leaving Tokyo. The mountain appears suddenly above the tree line and disappears within a minute or two. If it's overcast at departure, it often appears above the cloud layer — don't give up early.
